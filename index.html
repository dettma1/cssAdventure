Background Information


Why the focus on girls?

There are many reasons more women aren't in tech, which will be explored in the next section. However, I believe the absence of women is due to a lack of exposure to technology concepts when females are younger. This is why my game is targeted towards middle school girls.

Why do we need Women?

The first health app created by apple didn't have a period tracker. The first airbags were all created using male test dummies. My Google Home, which has been calibrated to my own voice, listens better to my male friends. If we want a world with inclusive technology, we all need to be represented when tech is being created.

Why a video game?

Video games are incredibly easy to access for the majority of students. A video game will allow girls to be exposed to new concepts quickly, and if they continue to play to game, then they can interact with material they may have never interacted with before.



Historical Women in Computing

Ada Lovelace

Grace Hopper

Katherine Johnson, Dorothy Vaughan, and Mary Jackson

The First Computer Programmer

Ada Lovelace was born in the 1800s. In her mid 30s she worked with Charles Babbage, known as the father of computing. She wrote an algorithm designed to be carried out by the machine Babbage was working on. Her manuscript is titled "Notes" and is regarded as the first computer program, making Lovelace the first computer programmer.

Grace Hopper

Grace Hopper is famous for coining the term "bug" (when code doesn't run correctly) when she found a moth in her own computer. Hopper was working in the Navy on computing when she suggested a new programming language be invented that used entirely English words. She was told computers couldn't understand english and the idea was absurd. Hopper then invented the first compiler, which takes human readable code and translates it into machine readable code.

The First Computers

Before the computer we know existed, women who excelled in math and science were hired to compute mathematical formulas. Katherine Johnson, Dorothy Vaughan, and Mary Jackson were all women who worked on the Apollo Mission. Their stories were made famous by the book and movie Hidden Figures. All three women worked extremely hard for their place in their field and were often denied recognization for their work because of their gender and race.

Women Representation

In a study conducted by the AAUW, in 2013 women made up 26% of computing professionals. This is actually down from 1930, the highest percentage of women in computing, around 35%.

Many girls who are interested in math and science tend to take less of these electives in middle school. They need more exposure to STEM jobs, female role models, and career awareness and planning."

Games Girls Play

According to the book Games Girls Play young girls enjoy games that allow them to use teamwork, have story telling components, and are connected to activities they already enjoy. They are more drawn to games without clear winners.

CS Coursework

Many women begin college without previous computing knowledge. Entering into the same learning environment with more advance students is discouraging for women, and an problem Harvey Mudd hoped to solve in partnership with the AnitaB.org Institute.

Diverse Educators

The majority of CS educators have either little computing experience or little teaching experience. The majority of professors are also men, and tend to teach how they learn best. Many professors were not new to computing when entering college and have a hard time helping and understanding new students.

Connecting it to a Game

What is CSS?

CSS is a tool used for creating websites with HTML. CSS stands for cascading style sheet and allows you to adjust background colors, positions, and appearances of objects on a webpage. I initially decided to use CSS because it is easy to see how objects change with CSS.

Adventure VS Puzzle Games

I want CSS Adventure to be whimsical and visually appealing, but also have more of a puzzle feel. The game isn't about beating others or getting high scores, the main objective is to creatively solve problems.

Introduction of Coding?

I also wanted CSS Adventure to provide an foundation for girls to learn computer science concepts. Editing code is a great start, but I hope to integrate smaller portions of code as well, and build up to writing small Python programs.

CSS Adventure



Characters

I wanted the main characters to be fun, somewhat quirky, but also something girls could identify with. I choose a mermaid and a manatee as the two main characters to play as for some variety.

Characters

In many video games female characters act as the damsel in distress. I wanted to keep a character who needed help, so I picked a manatee. In the current gameplay, after the fish is returned to the manatee, it is revealed the manatee is actually a king, and has quite a bit more help he needs to save the kindgom.

Characters

Many story-telling games feature enemies, or bad characters. In this case, the mermaid and manatee are working to protect their kingdom and keep it hidden from the humans, symbolized by the submarine. I really like having the enemy be a submarine, because it does not work as a personal attack on any group of people (expect many submarine seafarers). While the manatee and mermaid are working against someone else, the game is not centered on violence. Instead, they must rely on trickery, innovation, and creativity to outwit the submarine.


Gameplay

The game has minimal instructions and is meant to be played through a process of exploration. I love computer science because you are able to quickly try out new ideas and adjust them along the way. This idea incorporated through moving the blue fish and trying to find the location of the manatee. Currently, the game only has one "puzzle," but the idea would be to expand the game to a series of puzzles that create a narrative together. They would increase in difficulty and the ending result would be involve some python code or scratch-like coding exercise of putting code into the correct order. By the end of the game, I am hoping girls will have more of an understanding of how computer science works, and the creativity and problem-solving necessary.
